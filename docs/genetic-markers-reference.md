# Genetic Markers Reference

NutriGene analyzes **11 genetic targets** — 10 individual SNPs across 10 genes, plus one compound test that combines two SNPs to determine your APOE type. This reference explains what each gene does, what the variants mean, and why they matter for nutrition.

---

## How to Use This Reference

Each entry below includes:
- The gene name and the specific SNP tested (rsID)
- The biological pathway it belongs to
- What the gene normally does
- What the variants mean for your body
- Why it matters nutritionally

For impact levels and your personal genotype, refer to the SNP cards in the dashboard. This reference provides the scientific background behind each marker.

## Methylation & B-Vitamins Pathway

### MTHFR C677T
**rsID:** rs1801133

**What this gene does:**
MTHFR (methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase) produces an enzyme that converts folate (vitamin B9) into its active form, 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF). This active folate is essential for a process called methylation — a fundamental biochemical reaction involved in DNA repair, detoxification, neurotransmitter production, and homocysteine regulation.

**The C677T variant:**
The T allele at this position causes the MTHFR enzyme to function at reduced efficiency:
- One T allele (CT genotype): approximately 35-40% reduced enzyme activity
- Two T alleles (TT genotype): approximately 70% reduced enzyme activity

Reduced activity means less active folate is produced, which can lead to elevated homocysteine levels in the blood — a marker associated with cardiovascular and neurological risk.

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
People with the TT or CT genotype may not convert synthetic folic acid (found in fortified foods and many supplements) efficiently. The nutritional strategy is to supplement with **methylfolate (5-MTHF)** — the pre-converted active form — rather than folic acid. Adequate riboflavin (B2) also supports MTHFR enzyme function. Homocysteine levels can be monitored with a standard blood test.

---

### MTHFR A1298C
**rsID:** rs1801131

**What this gene does:**
This is a second variant in the same MTHFR gene, at a different position. While C677T primarily affects folate-to-methylfolate conversion, A1298C affects a different part of the enzyme's function — specifically the production of BH4 (tetrahydrobiopterin), a cofactor required for synthesizing dopamine and serotonin.

**The A1298C variant:**
The C allele at this position reduces the enzyme's ability to support BH4 synthesis. This pathway feeds into neurotransmitter production. Effect sizes are generally smaller than C677T, but the impact can be compounded in people who also carry C677T variants.

- One C allele (AC genotype): mildly reduced BH4-related function
- Two C alleles (CC genotype): more noticeably reduced function

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
This variant is associated with mood-related pathways due to its influence on dopamine and serotonin precursor synthesis. Nutritional support strategies often include methylfolate, B12 (as methylcobalamin), and precursor nutrients for neurotransmitters. The combination of MTHFR C677T and A1298C compound heterozygosity (one copy of each variant) is particularly relevant and worth discussing with a healthcare provider.

### COMT Val158Met
**rsID:** rs4680

**What this gene does:**
COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase) encodes an enzyme that breaks down catecholamines — primarily dopamine, epinephrine, and norepinephrine — in the prefrontal cortex and other tissues. It is one of the primary regulators of dopamine clearance in the brain.

**The Val158Met variant:**
- The **Val allele (G)** produces a more active enzyme: faster dopamine breakdown, lower baseline dopamine levels
- The **Met allele (A)** produces a less active enzyme: slower dopamine breakdown, higher baseline dopamine levels

Neither allele is inherently "better." High dopamine supports working memory and focus under normal conditions, but can make individuals more sensitive to stress. Low dopamine is associated with better stress resilience but may affect motivation and reward processing.

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
COMT requires SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) as a methyl donor, which is fed by the methylation cycle (making MTHFR variants relevant here too). Magnesium is a COMT cofactor. People with the Met/Met (slow COMT) genotype may be more sensitive to high-catecholamine states and may benefit from stress management alongside nutritional support.

## Vitamin D & Bone Density Pathway

### VDR BsmI
**rsID:** rs1544410

**What this gene does:**
VDR (vitamin D receptor) encodes the receptor protein that binds to active vitamin D (calcitriol) inside cells. When vitamin D binds to VDR, it activates genes involved in calcium absorption, immune regulation, bone density maintenance, and many other processes. The efficiency of this receptor directly affects how much biological work your vitamin D actually does — regardless of your blood levels.

**The BsmI variant:**
The BsmI polymorphism affects VDR gene expression levels — how many receptor proteins are made. The B allele (A at rs1544410) is associated with higher receptor expression and better receptor efficiency; the b allele (G) with lower expression.

- BB (AA genotype): Higher VDR expression — optimal vitamin D signaling
- Bb (AG genotype): Intermediate receptor efficiency
- bb (GG genotype): Lower VDR expression — reduced vitamin D signaling efficiency

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
Even with adequate blood levels of vitamin D (25-OH-D), someone with reduced VDR efficiency may not get the full benefit. People with the bb genotype are often advised to maintain higher vitamin D levels through supplementation and sun exposure, and to support absorption with vitamin K2 and magnesium, which work alongside vitamin D in the bone and cardiovascular systems.

## Lipid & Heart Integrity Pathway

### APOE (Compound Test)
**rsIDs:** rs429358 + rs7412

**What this gene does:**
APOE (apolipoprotein E) is a protein involved in lipid transport — it helps carry cholesterol and triglycerides through the bloodstream and delivers fats to cells. It is also involved in brain cholesterol management and neural repair. The APOE gene is unusual in that your "type" is determined by the combination of two SNPs rather than just one.

**The three APOE alleles:**

By combining the results at rs429358 and rs7412, the app determines whether you carry the E2, E3, or E4 allele at each of your two chromosomes. This gives you one of six possible genotypes (E2/E2, E2/E3, E2/E4, E3/E3, E3/E4, or E4/E4).

| Allele | Frequency | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **E2** | ~8% of population | Associated with lower LDL cholesterol; rare association with Type III hyperlipoproteinemia |
| **E3** | ~78% of population | The most common "neutral" allele; considered the baseline |
| **E4** | ~14% of population | Associated with higher LDL cholesterol, reduced cholesterol clearance, and elevated Alzheimer's risk |

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
APOE4 carriers benefit most from dietary fat management — specifically reducing saturated fat intake and increasing omega-3 fatty acids. The E4 allele is less efficient at clearing LDL cholesterol from the bloodstream and is associated with faster cognitive aging. Nutritional strategies for E4 carriers often emphasize Mediterranean-style diets, DHA supplementation (omega-3), antioxidants (vitamins C and E), and careful monitoring of cardiovascular markers. APOE2 carriers may paradoxically benefit from some dietary fat to manage their specific lipid profile.

## Dietary Tolerances & Caffeine Pathway

### MCM6
**rsID:** rs4988235

**What this gene does:**
MCM6 is located near the LCT gene, which encodes lactase — the enzyme that digests lactose (milk sugar). Most mammals stop producing lactase after weaning. In humans, a genetic mutation near MCM6 can keep lactase production active into adulthood. This is called lactase persistence.

**The rs4988235 variant:**
- **A allele (T):** Associated with lactase persistence — continued production of lactase throughout adult life, allowing comfortable digestion of dairy
- **G allele (C):** Associated with lactase non-persistence — lactase production declines after childhood, leading to lactose intolerance in many people

- AA genotype: Likely lactose tolerant
- AG genotype: Variable — may tolerate moderate dairy; partial lactase activity
- GG genotype: Likely lactose intolerant or dairy-sensitive

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
Knowing your lactase persistence status helps guide dairy consumption. People with the GG genotype may benefit from lactase enzyme supplements when consuming dairy, choosing lactose-free dairy products, or sourcing calcium and other dairy nutrients from alternative foods (leafy greens, fortified plant milks, canned fish with bones).

### CYP1A2
**rsID:** rs762551

**What this gene does:**
CYP1A2 is a liver enzyme responsible for metabolizing caffeine (and several other compounds including some medications). How fast this enzyme works determines whether caffeine is cleared quickly or lingers in your system.

**The rs762551 variant:**
- **A allele:** Associated with fast caffeine metabolism — caffeine is broken down and cleared quickly
- **C allele:** Associated with slow caffeine metabolism — caffeine lingers longer in the bloodstream

- AA genotype: Fast metabolizer — caffeine effects wear off quickly, lower cardiovascular risk from caffeine
- AC genotype: Intermediate metabolizer
- CC genotype: Slow metabolizer — prolonged caffeine exposure; associated with higher cardiovascular risk from regular coffee consumption in some studies

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
Slow CYP1A2 metabolizers may experience more pronounced effects from caffeine (jitteriness, disrupted sleep, elevated heart rate) and may benefit from limiting intake. Research also suggests slow metabolizers who consume high amounts of coffee may have a modestly elevated risk of heart attack, though the absolute risk is small. Knowing your status helps you make informed decisions about caffeine consumption.

## Vitamin A / B12 Gut Absorption Pathway

### BCMO1 R267S
**rsID:** rs6564851

**What this gene does:**
BCMO1 (beta-carotene monooxygenase 1) encodes the enzyme that converts beta-carotene — the orange pigment found in carrots, sweet potatoes, and leafy greens — into retinol (active vitamin A). This conversion is how most people obtain vitamin A from plant foods.

**The R267S variant:**
- **G allele (Arg/R):** Normal BCMO1 enzyme activity — efficient beta-carotene conversion
- **A allele (Ser/S):** Reduced BCMO1 activity — impaired conversion of beta-carotene to vitamin A

- GG genotype: Efficient conversion
- GA genotype: Moderately reduced conversion (approximately 32% reduction in some studies)
- AA genotype: Significantly reduced conversion — heavy reliance on plant-source beta-carotene may be insufficient for vitamin A needs

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
People with reduced BCMO1 function cannot rely on beta-carotene-rich plant foods as a reliable vitamin A source. The practical solution is to ensure adequate intake of **pre-formed vitamin A (retinol)** — found in animal foods such as eggs, dairy, liver, and fatty fish. Vegan and vegetarian individuals with this variant should pay particular attention to their vitamin A status.

---

### FUT2
**rsID:** rs601338

**What this gene does:**
FUT2 (fucosyltransferase 2) is an enzyme that determines secretor status — whether you secrete blood group antigens into body fluids including the gut lining. This affects the composition and diversity of your gut microbiome, particularly which bacteria can colonize the intestinal mucosa. Secretors provide a binding surface that beneficial bacteria (especially Bifidobacteria) use to attach to the gut wall. The composition of your microbiome, in turn, affects how well you absorb certain nutrients — particularly vitamin B12.

**The rs601338 variant:**
- **A allele:** Non-secretor — lower Bifidobacteria diversity, impaired gut-mediated B12 absorption
- **G allele:** Secretor — supports a more diverse gut microbiome favorable to B12 absorption

- AA genotype: Non-secretor — most impaired B12 absorption via gut microbiome
- AG genotype: One secretor allele — intermediate
- GG genotype: Secretor — typical B12 absorption capacity

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
Non-secretors may absorb dietary vitamin B12 less efficiently, even from B12-rich foods. Supplementation with vitamin B12 (as methylcobalamin or adenosylcobalamin rather than cyanocobalamin) is often recommended. Probiotic support targeting Bifidobacteria strains may also help maintain a healthy gut microbiome. B12 deficiency is associated with fatigue, neurological symptoms, and elevated homocysteine — making this variant nutritionally significant, particularly for older adults and those on plant-based diets.

---

### NBPF3
**rsID:** rs4654748

**What this gene does:**
NBPF3 (neuroblastoma breakpoint family member 3) has been identified in genome-wide association studies as influencing circulating vitamin B6 (pyridoxal-5-phosphate / PLP) levels. The exact mechanism involves B6 clearance rate — how quickly the body removes active vitamin B6 from circulation.

**The rs4654748 variant:**
- **C allele:** Associated with higher circulating B6 levels — normal retention
- **T allele:** Associated with lower circulating B6 levels — faster B6 clearance

- CC genotype: Typical B6 retention
- CT genotype: Mildly accelerated clearance
- TT genotype: Fastest clearance — lowest circulating B6

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
Vitamin B6 is a cofactor in over 100 enzymatic reactions, including neurotransmitter synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, GABA), hemoglobin formation, and immune function. Low B6 is associated with depression, PMS symptoms, and poor immune response. People with the TT genotype may need higher dietary B6 intake or supplementation to maintain adequate levels. Active B6 (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) supplements bypass the conversion step and may be more effective for those with clearance issues.

---

### FADS1
**rsID:** rs174537

**What this gene does:**
FADS1 (fatty acid desaturase 1) encodes an enzyme that converts short-chain omega-3 fatty acids — primarily ALA (alpha-linolenic acid, found in flaxseed, walnuts, and chia seeds) — into the longer-chain EPA and DHA that the body actually uses for brain function, inflammation regulation, and cardiovascular health. This conversion is the critical step that determines how much benefit you get from plant-based omega-3 sources.

**The rs174537 variant:**
- **G allele:** Higher FADS1 enzyme activity — more efficient ALA-to-EPA/DHA conversion
- **T allele:** Lower FADS1 enzyme activity — poor conversion of plant-source omega-3s into usable long-chain forms

- GG genotype: Efficient conversion — can rely more on plant sources of omega-3
- GT genotype: Intermediate conversion
- TT genotype: Poor conversion — plant-source omega-3s provide little actual EPA/DHA

**Why it matters nutritionally:**
People with reduced FADS1 activity cannot efficiently convert flaxseed, walnuts, or chia seeds into the brain and heart-protective EPA and DHA. The practical implication is direct supplementation with **pre-formed long-chain omega-3s** — from fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) or algae-based DHA/EPA supplements. This is particularly important for vegetarians and vegans who rely exclusively on plant-source ALA.
